CTS v2 — Full Formula Specification

The Compound Threat Score captures nonlinear interactions between attack properties. Unlike simple additive scoring, CTS uses multiplicative cores — because an attack that is persistent and stealthy and autonomous is qualitatively more dangerous than the sum of those properties suggests.

The Master Formula

CTS = φ(P, Pr, Au) × ψ(S, O) × ω(A, Pr) + λ(T, R) + ΣI

Three multiplicative cores (φ × ψ × ω) capture whether the attack is self-sustaining, invisible, and scalable. Two additive terms (λ + ΣI) add forensic difficulty and pairwise synergy bonuses. The result is normalized to a 0–10 scale.

φ — Sustainability Core

φ = 1 + (P × Pr0.7 × Au) / 19.44 × 4

What it measures: Can the attack keep itself alive and spreading without the attacker doing anything more?

  • P (Persistence) — does it survive across sessions?
  • Pr (Propagation) — does it spread to other agents? Uses Pr0.7 (concave) because the jump from "can't spread" to "can spread at all" matters most.
  • Au (Autonomy) — does it run on its own?

These three multiply because persistence without autonomy needs manual upkeep, autonomy without persistence dies each session, and propagation without either is a flash in the pan. All three together = autonomous epidemic. Range: 1.0 – 5.0

ψ — Evasion Core

ψ = 1 + (S × O) / 9 × 3

What it measures: Can any layer of defense — automated scanners, critic models, or human reviewers — actually detect this attack?

  • S (Stealth) — how hard is it to spot?
  • O (Oversight Defeat) — does it beat all oversight layers?

These multiply because high stealth but weak oversight defeat means automated systems catch it; strong oversight defeat but low stealth means basic scanning finds it. Both together = invisible to everything. Range: 1.0 – 4.0

ω — Scale Core

ω = 1 + (A × 0.7 × Pr) / 9 × 2

What it measures: How big does the blast radius get?

  • A (Amplification) — does the effect grow beyond the initial input?
  • Pr (Propagation) — how many agents are hit? Uses 0.7 × Pr (attenuated) because Pr already contributes to φ.

Range: 1.0 – 3.0

λ — Forensic Penalty (additive)

λ = 2 × (T + R)
  • T (Temporal Separation) — delay between planting and effect complicates attribution
  • R (Remediation Resistance) — how hard is cleanup once detected?

These are additive, not multiplicative — they make a bad attack worse but don't qualitatively transform it. Range: 0 – 12

ΣI — Interaction Bonuses (additive)

Specific factor pairs that create emergent dangers when both are ≥ 2:

PairBonusWhy
P × S+2Persistent + Stealthy = may never be discovered
Au × T+2Autonomous + Delayed = self-executing time bomb
O × R+3Can't detect it + can't fix it = effectively permanent
Pr × A+2Spreading + Amplifying = epidemic threshold
S × T+2Invisible + Delayed = forensic nightmare

Maximum interaction bonus: +11

Normalization

CTS_normalized = (Raw_CTS − 1) / (71 − 1) × 10

Raw CTS ranges from 1.0 (all zeros — the three cores each equal 1, product = 1) to 71.0 (all factors at 3). This is linearly mapped to 0.0 – 10.0 for CVSS compatibility.

CxTS — Contextual Threat Score (Mitigation)

M = 1 − (Rev_inv × D_inv) / 9 × 0.6
CxTS = CTS × M

The Mitigation Factor M accounts for real-world context that reduces effective risk:

  • Rev (Reversibility) — 0 = irreversible (worst) → 3 = fully reversible (best, most mitigating)
  • D (Domain Criticality) — 0 = critical stakes (worst) → 3 = low stakes (best, most mitigating)

Key insight: These sliders represent defenses and mitigations, not additional threats. Higher values = more mitigation = lower CxTS. When both are 0 (irreversible harm in a critical domain), M = 1.0 and CxTS = CTS (no mitigation). When both are 3 (fully reversible in a low-stakes domain), M = 0.4 and CxTS = 0.4 × CTS (maximum mitigation).

M ranges from 1.0× (no mitigation — worst case) to 0.4× (maximum mitigation — best case). CxTS ≤ CTS always.
